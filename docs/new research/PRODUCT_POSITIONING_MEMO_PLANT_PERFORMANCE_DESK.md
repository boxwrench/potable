# Product Positioning Memo: Plant Performance Desk

Date: 2026-04-29
Status: Working positioning memo

## Purpose

This memo captures the emerging commercial concept for Operational Inference after the process-screening-first strategy discussion.

The goal is to distinguish between:

- the first pilot artifact
- the first sellable offer
- the long-term subscription product
- the larger platform direction

The central clarification is:

`The basin screening memo is the first proof artifact. It is not the whole business.`

## Core Concept

Operational Inference gives small and mid-sized water systems access to expert-backed treatment decision support, practical screening tools, and clear next-step guidance so they can make good decisions faster and get back to making water.

The product is not just a basin screener, a report shop, a compliance tool, or a generic AI copilot.

The product is a practical plant-performance support layer for teams that do not have large internal engineering benches, dedicated process specialists, or expensive modeling tools available for everyday questions.

## Buyer Truth

The buyer does not want more software to babysit.

The buyer wants the right information at the right time, in language they can use with:

- operators
- GMs
- public works directors
- boards
- consultants
- regulators
- maintenance staff

The operator or manager wants to make a good call, explain it clearly, and move on.

They want to get back to making water.

## Emotional Promise

Operational Inference should help the buyer feel:

- clearer
- faster
- better prepared
- less alone on hard process questions
- more credible in front of leadership

The practical emotional promise:

`This helps you look smart to your GM because you can explain what changed, what to check, what to try, and when to call engineering.`

That promise matters because many small and mid-sized systems are expected to answer big-system questions without big-system staff or tooling.

## Product Category

The category should be framed as:

- water-treatment decision support
- plant-performance support
- expert-backed process screening
- practical treatment intelligence for small and mid-sized systems

The product should not be framed primarily as:

- cheap engineering reports
- generic AI for water
- a chatbot for operators
- a compliance platform
- a digital twin
- CFD replacement
- autonomous treatment optimization

## Primary Product

Working product name:

`Plant Performance Desk`

Product definition:

`Plant Performance Desk is a subscription-style decision-support service and software layer that helps water-treatment teams answer process questions, interpret plant behavior, generate screening outputs, and decide what to do next.`

The buyer should experience it as:

- a place to bring treatment-process questions
- a system that remembers plant context
- a source of structured expert-backed answers
- a tool for generating leadership-ready explanations
- a lightweight alternative to turning every question into a full engineering project

## Core Promise

Short version:

`Make better treatment decisions faster, then get back to making water.`

Longer version:

`Operational Inference helps small and mid-sized water systems answer hard treatment-process questions quickly, clearly, and defensibly, so operators and managers can make good decisions faster and get back to making water.`

Operational version:

`Know what changed, what to check, what to try, and when to call engineering.`

## First Technical Wedge

The first technical wedge remains:

- sedimentation design-vs-current screening
- plant-performance diagnosis
- comparison of intended versus current behavior
- structured escalation to field verification or engineering review

This wedge is credible because it is:

- founder-fit
- already supported by `sed_model22`
- visually and analytically demonstrable
- differentiated from generic compliance software
- valuable as a proof artifact for larger plant-performance support

But it should be positioned as the first module inside the larger support system, not as the full company category.

## First Pilot Artifact

The first pilot artifact is:

`Plant Performance Screening Memo`

This memo may look like a cheaper engineering report, but it serves a different strategic function.

It is used to:

- prove the workflow
- show the buyer the end artifact before purchase
- generate structured feedback
- capture expert decision reasoning
- create an anonymized case package
- test whether screening pilots can strengthen Potable/PotableLM dataset development

The memo is the artifact that earns trust.

It is not the final business model.

## Business Model View

### What is one-time?

One-time offers:

- validation pilot
- paid plant-performance screening engagement
- focused design-vs-current screening memo
- deep-dive process question
- consultant handoff package

These are useful for trust-building, cash, and case development.

They should stay product-shaped, not open-ended consulting.

### What is subscription?

Subscription offers:

- Plant Performance Desk
- ongoing question intake
- recurring plant-performance review
- retained plant context
- scenario updates
- leadership-ready short memos
- screening modules as they mature
- structured issue history
- annual or quarterly support package

The subscription is not "pay monthly for the basin screener."

The subscription is:

`pay monthly for expert-backed treatment decision support and practical screening capacity that your system could not otherwise afford to keep in-house.`

### What is standalone software?

Standalone software should come later.

It becomes appropriate when:

- the repeated workflow is known
- users can self-serve without misusing the output
- caution and escalation language is built into the product
- the support desk has revealed which features are actually used
- the model/report outputs are polished enough to stand alone

Standalone software with updates may eventually exist, but it should not be the first commercial posture.

## Offer Ladder

Recommended sequence:

1. validation pilot
2. paid screening engagement
3. Plant Performance Desk subscription
4. multi-module plant-performance platform
5. consultant / engineering partner version

### 1. Validation Pilot

Price:

- `$0-$3,500`

Purpose:

- feedback
- case-study rights
- recorded intake and review calls with permission
- anonymized research and dataset rights
- product validation

Use when:

- the buyer is trusted
- learning value is high
- public or anonymized case value is meaningful

### 2. Paid Screening Engagement

Price:

- `$7,500-$20,000`

Purpose:

- fixed-scope plant-performance question
- design-vs-current memo
- review session
- next-step recommendation
- relationship to future subscription

Use when:

- the buyer has a serious unresolved question
- confidentiality is important
- the buyer wants private client value

### 3. Plant Performance Desk Subscription

Possible early pricing hypothesis:

- `$500-$1,500/month` for small-system support
- `$2,000-$5,000/month` for standard plant-performance support
- higher custom pricing for multi-plant or consultant use

Included value may include:

- defined question intake
- defined response times
- monthly or quarterly process review
- short memos or decision notes
- plant context record
- scenario screening where available
- escalation recommendations
- "explain this to my GM" summaries

Important boundary:

Do not literally promise unlimited 24/7 expert command unless the operation is staffed and insured for that. Promise structured support with clear response expectations.

### 4. Multi-Module Platform

Potential modules:

- sedimentation screening
- filtration performance interpretation
- coagulation/flocculation troubleshooting
- disinfection/CT support
- seasonal performance comparisons
- field verification planning
- process issue history
- consultant handoff packages
- selected compliance/performance reporting layers

The platform grows from real customer questions, not from a speculative feature list.

### 5. Consultant / Engineering Partner Version

Potential later offer:

- pre-study screening
- reusable client-facing memo generation
- scenario comparison outputs
- internal triage before deeper CFD, tracer, or design work

This may become a strong secondary channel because consultants already sell diagnosis and may value faster scoping.

## Positioning Lines

Use and test:

- `Make better treatment decisions faster.`
- `Get back to making water.`
- `Big-utility tools at small-system prices.`
- `Ask the treatment question. Get the reasoning. Know the next step.`
- `Know what changed, what to check, what to try, and when to call engineering.`

Use carefully:

- `Ask the smartest or dumbest question you can think of.`

This is emotionally strong but should be made professionally safe in outward copy:

`Bring the simple questions, the hard questions, and the messy in-between questions.`

Avoid:

- `AI plant operator`
- `autonomous optimization`
- `digital twin`
- `engineering replacement`
- `24/7 expert team` unless operationally true
- `cheap engineering report`

## Buyer Segments

### Best Early Buyer

Small to mid-sized conventional surface-water systems with:

- real treatment complexity
- limited internal engineering capacity
- a plant manager or superintendent who values process reasoning
- enough budget for support but not enough staff for a full internal expert bench
- warm relationship access

### Strong Secondary Buyer

Consulting engineers who need:

- fast pre-study screening
- better client-facing explanation artifacts
- triage before expensive modeling or field work
- repeatable first-pass memo generation

### Poor First Buyer

Avoid optimizing first for:

- very small systems with no budget
- large enterprise utilities with long procurement
- state regulators
- emergency response contexts
- buyers who only want generic compliance reporting

## What The Buyer Gets

The buyer gets:

- clear answers to treatment-process questions
- structured next steps
- screening outputs where the data supports them
- leadership-ready explanation
- support deciding whether a problem is hydraulic, chemical, operational, maintenance-related, or engineering-worthy
- a record of reasoning over time
- access to tools they would not normally have at their scale

They are not buying:

- final engineering design
- PE-stamped recommendations
- emergency operational command
- compliance certification
- autonomous control
- generic chatbot output

## Trust Model

Trust comes from:

- visible assumptions
- clear confidence tiers
- explicit caution language
- practical operator voice
- repeatable memo structure
- transparent escalation boundaries
- willingness to say "this is inconclusive"

The product should earn trust by being honest about:

- what is screening
- what is proxy
- what needs field verification
- what belongs with an engineer
- what should not be changed from the memo alone

## Dataset And Product Learning

The Plant Performance Desk can become a data engine if designed deliberately.

High-value capture points:

- intake question
- cues that triggered concern
- hypotheses already considered
- model output the client trusted
- model output the client questioned
- model output the client rejected
- next step the client actually took

This supports Potable/PotableLM because it captures real expert reasoning around actual plant questions.

The capture must be permissioned and contractually clear.

Free or nominal pilots should buy:

- structured feedback
- anonymized reasoning rights
- case-study potential
- dataset learning

Paid confidential pilots may not produce the same public or dataset value.

## Main Strategic Risk

The main risk is becoming a low-priced consulting/report shop.

Avoid this by keeping every engagement:

- fixed-scope
- repeatable
- artifact-driven
- structured around reusable workflow
- connected to subscription support
- tied to product learning

The service work should train the product. It should not replace it.

## Current Recommendation

Proceed with this commercial architecture:

1. Build a completed mock `Plant Performance Screening Memo`.
2. Use that memo to open one trusted validation conversation.
3. Offer a free or nominal validation pilot in exchange for feedback, recorded review, and anonymized learning rights.
4. Use the pilot to test both the deliverable and the Plant Performance Desk subscription thesis.
5. If the pilot works, convert the next conversation toward either a paid screening engagement or a lightweight monthly support desk.

The north star should remain:

`Operational Inference helps water-treatment teams make good decisions faster and get back to making water.`
