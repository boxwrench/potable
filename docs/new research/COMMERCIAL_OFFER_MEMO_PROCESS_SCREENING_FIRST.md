# Commercial Offer Memo: Process-Screening First

Date: 2026-04-29
Status: Working commercial recommendation

## Purpose

This memo translates the strategic recommendation in [DECISION_MEMO_PROCESS_SCREENING_FIRST.md](C:/Github/potable/docs/new%20research/DECISION_MEMO_PROCESS_SCREENING_FIRST.md) into an actual commercial starting point.

It answers four questions:

1. Who buys first
2. What exact problem statement is sold
3. What the first paid pilot should be
4. What proof is required before charging meaningful money

## Commercial Starting Point

The first commercial offer should not be "software access" in the abstract.
It should be a **screening engagement with software inside it**.

That is the right starting shape because:

- trust is not yet strong enough for pure self-serve SaaS
- the product is still proving where the model is strongest
- the buyer is paying for faster, clearer diagnosis of a real plant question
- the engagement creates the case material needed to later productize

So the first thing sold is not:

- a basin simulator license
- a digital twin subscription
- AI for operators

It is:

- a structured plant-performance screening engagement
- delivered with transparent outputs
- aimed at a specific process question
- with explicit scope boundaries and handoff logic

## First Buyer

### Primary buyer

The best first buyer is likely:

- plant manager
- treatment superintendent
- engineering manager

at a conventional surface-water treatment plant large enough to care about process-performance diagnosis but small enough to move faster than a top-tier enterprise utility.

Ideal profile:

- conventional surface-water plant
- recurring or unresolved sedimentation / filter-performance concerns
- enough internal technical literacy to understand a screening output
- enough budget to approve a limited pilot without a full RFP ordeal
- operational culture that respects process diagnosis, not just compliance minimums

Practical target range:

- medium to upper-mid market municipal plants
- systems with real process complexity but not the procurement drag of the largest agencies

### Secondary buyer

A strong secondary buyer is:

- consulting engineer

especially a mid-market process engineer or water consultant who routinely faces "is this worth deeper study?" questions but cannot justify full CFD or long custom study cycles on every job.

This buyer matters because:

- they already sell diagnosis
- they understand the value of first-pass screening
- they may adopt earlier than a utility if the tool helps them scope work faster

### Buyer to avoid first

Do not optimize first for:

- very small systems
- solo operators
- state regulators
- large enterprise utility IT-led buyers

Those segments either lack budget, require too much simplification, or create long procurement cycles too early.

## First Problem Statement

The first problem statement should be extremely narrow and concrete.

### Recommended first problem statement

"We help water-treatment plants compare design intent versus current behavior in sedimentation-related process performance, so they can decide whether they are dealing with a hydraulics problem, a chemistry problem, an operational problem, or a condition that warrants deeper engineering review."

That is the most commercially useful version of what `sed_model22` is already trying to become.

### Why this problem statement works

It is:

- specific enough to feel real
- broad enough to cover multiple practical plant questions
- narrow enough to avoid becoming generic consulting language
- aligned with the repo's actual model boundary

It also avoids the two worst traps:

- claiming full design authority
- sounding like a vague analytics platform

### Alternate phrasing for buyers

For utilities:

"We provide first-pass process screening that helps your team understand whether current basin behavior is materially different from design intent and what to investigate next."

For consultants:

"We provide rapid pre-study screening for sedimentation-related plant questions so your team can determine whether a site warrants deeper engineering modeling or field validation."

## First Paid Pilot

### Recommended pilot shape

The first paid pilot should be a **fixed-scope design-vs-current screening engagement** for one plant or one basin question.

It should include:

- intake and geometry capture
- current-condition scenario framing
- screening run and comparison outputs
- short written interpretation
- live review session
- explicit next-step recommendation

### What the buyer receives

Deliverables should include:

- a concise engineering screening memo
- visuals comparing design-intent and current-state scenarios
- clear statement of likely process implications
- confidence and limitation language
- recommended follow-up actions

Recommended action types:

- no immediate action, continue monitoring
- field verification needed
- operational trial worth attempting
- consultant study warranted
- CFD / tracer / more detailed analysis warranted

### What the pilot should not promise

The pilot should not promise:

- stamped engineering judgment
- final design recommendation
- guaranteed operational savings
- autonomous control recommendation
- full solids or water-quality prediction beyond the current validated boundary

### Suggested first pilot packaging

Pilot name:

- `Plant Performance Screening Pilot`

Scope:

- one conventional surface-water process question
- one basin or one process train segment
- one design-vs-current comparison package

Format:

- fixed fee
- fixed timeline
- explicit assumptions up front

### Suggested pricing and validation tradeoff

Initial commercial pilot pricing should likely be:

- low enough to reduce procurement resistance
- high enough to signal real professional value

Practical starting band:

- $7,500 to $20,000 for the first pilot

However, the first one or two pilots may be better structured as validation pilots rather than normal paid pilots.

Use this tradeoff:

- free or nominal cost if the client grants structured feedback, recorded intake/review calls with permission, anonymized research use, and possible anonymized case-study rights
- paid pilot pricing if the client wants fully private work with no research, dataset, reference, or case-study value

Nominal-cost validation pricing could be:

- $0 for a highly trusted validation partner with strong feedback and case-study rights
- $1,500 to $3,500 if a small fee creates commitment without procurement friction
- $7,500+ once the work is confidential, private, or clearly commercial

The important rule:

- free should buy learning and rights
- paid should buy confidentiality and client control

Lower end of the paid band if:

- first validation customer
- relationship-based access
- case-study rights included

Upper end if:

- buyer has urgent unresolved question
- cleaner data access exists
- review audience includes management or engineering leadership

The first few pilots should be priced for learning plus credibility, not margin maximization.

## Proof Required Before Charging Real Money

There are two different thresholds:

1. proof needed to charge pilot money
2. proof needed to charge meaningful recurring product money

### Proof needed for pilot money

Before charging for a first limited pilot, you need:

- a technically coherent workflow
- outputs that are legible to non-specialists
- honest model-boundary language
- at least one internally credible demonstration case

This threshold is not perfection.
It is enough confidence that the screening engagement is professionally responsible and decision-useful.

For your current project, that likely means:

- the `design-vs-current` workflow is stable
- geometry assumptions are explicit
- the visual and memo output can be reviewed by a plant manager or engineer without confusion
- the repo can produce repeatable outputs for the same scenario

### Proof needed for meaningful recurring money

Before charging substantial annual product fees, you need stronger evidence:

- at least 2 to 3 retrospective or live cases where the screening output is directionally right
- at least 1 customer who says the result changed, accelerated, or clarified a real decision
- evidence that the workflow is reusable beyond a single idiosyncratic plant
- cleaner output packaging than a research repo currently provides

That is the threshold where you can start moving from:

- paid screening engagement

toward:

- recurring product subscription
- annual plant-performance license
- consultant seat license

## Recommended Validation Sequence

### Phase 1: internal proof

Goal:

- prove the workflow is coherent and communicable

Needed:

- one or two strong demonstration cases
- one repeatable memo format
- one standard output package

### Phase 2: relationship-based pilot

Goal:

- prove a real plant or engineer will engage with the workflow

Needed:

- one first pilot customer
- willingness to give feedback
- ideally permission to anonymize and reuse learnings

### Phase 3: external proof of value

Goal:

- prove this is not just intellectually interesting

Needed evidence:

- "this helped us understand what was wrong"
- "this narrowed the next step"
- "this saved us time before calling a consultant"
- "this gave us a more defensible internal conversation"

This is the key commercial threshold.

## Recommended First Offer Language

Use language like:

"Operational Inference offers a plant-performance screening engagement for conventional water-treatment process questions. The engagement compares design intent and current-condition behavior, identifies likely hydraulic or process-performance concerns, and provides a structured basis for deciding whether further field verification or engineering review is warranted."

Do not use language like:

- AI-powered plant optimization
- digital twin for treatment operations
- compliance automation platform
- automated engineering design

## What Success Looks Like In The First 90 Days

A realistic near-term success target is not ARR.
It is commercial signal.

Success would look like:

- 5 to 10 serious conversations
- 2 to 3 plants or engineers willing to review the concept
- 1 paid pilot
- 1 strong anonymized case package
- 1 clearer statement of which process question buyers care about most

That would be enough to justify continuing down this wedge.

## The Most Important Discipline

Do not let the first offer become generic consulting.

The offer must stay product-shaped:

- repeatable workflow
- repeatable deliverable structure
- repeatable scenario logic
- repeatable interpretation format

Even if the first revenue looks like a service engagement, the internal operating model should behave like product development.

## Recommendation

The first commercial move should be:

- sell a fixed-scope plant-performance screening pilot
- aimed at sedimentation-related design-vs-current diagnosis
- to a plant manager, superintendent, engineering manager, or selected consultant
- with explicit handoff boundaries
- priced for learning and credibility

That is the cleanest bridge from the current `sed_model22` project state to a real business.
